### Oil and Gas Investment Hurdles Have Shifted Over Time (Return on Capital Employed)
In oil and gas, investment decisions must demonstrate that the project can clear the hurdle rate. The hurdle rate --- often set around 10--15% --- represents the minimum return on capital employed (ROCE) that companies require before committing billions of dollars to new exploration, production, or refining projects.

But as the chart above shows, the actual returns achieved by the industry's largest players have varied widely over time.

### The Long Arc of Returns
The chart tracks the ROCE for ExxonMobil, BP, TotalEnergies, Shell, and Chevron since the early 1990s. Several themes emerge:

- Volatility is the norm. Returns swing sharply in response to oil price cycles. The spikes in the 2000s reflect booming crude prices, while the troughs in 2009 and 2020 align with global crises.
- Leaders change over time. TotalEnergies currently sits at the top with a 15.2% ROCE, slightly above ExxonMobil at 14.0%. BP lags far behind at 6.0%, reflecting weaker capital discipline and portfolio challenges.
- The long-term average clusters near the hurdle rate. Despite wild swings, most companies gravitate toward the 10--15% range over time. This underlines how tightly tied capital returns are to industry structure and commodity prices.


### Why ROCE Matters
ROCE measures how efficiently a company turns capital into profit. For oil and gas, where projects require massive upfront spending and multi-decade horizons, it is the clearest signal of whether the business is creating value.

A ROCE above the hurdle rate means new investments are worthwhile. A ROCE below it suggests companies should defer projects, return cash to shareholders, or restructure portfolios.

### The Challenge of New Investments
Capital discipline has improved since the shale boom, with firms focusing on shareholder returns rather than unchecked growth. On the other hand, energy transition pressures mean companies must weigh oil and gas investments against renewables and low-carbon projects.

The hurdle rate still applies, but the risks are higher:

- Oil price volatility makes returns uncertain.
- Long-cycle megaprojects compete with short-cycle shale or renewable investments.
- ESG pressures and carbon regulations could erode future returns.

### Looking Ahead
The chart reminds us that while oil and gas has historically delivered double-digit ROCE in good years, it has also endured long periods of underperformance. The future of investment in the sector will hinge on whether companies can consistently deliver above-hurdle returns in a world of price volatility, capital scarcity, and climate-driven change.

For investors, the key question is not whether oil and gas projects can occasionally clear the hurdle rate --- they clearly can. The question is whether they can sustainably do so in the decade ahead.
